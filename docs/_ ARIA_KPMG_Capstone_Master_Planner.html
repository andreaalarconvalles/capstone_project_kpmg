Section 01 — Problem Statement

The challenge we are solving

This is the narrative anchor for the entire project. Every technical choice, every model, every agent capability ties back to this problem. It is what we pitch to KPMG and what every team member should be able to articulate in 60 seconds.

KPMG Lighthouse Capstone — Problem Statement

Short-term rental investment decisions are made on intuition.
They should be made on intelligence.

The European short-term rental market is growing at 11.5% CAGR and will reach $81 billion by 2030. Institutional and individual investors are increasingly deploying capital into urban STR assets across European cities — yet their decision-making tools remain a patchwork of static dashboards, manual research, and heuristic judgements.

Two fundamental problems exist simultaneously. First, pricing opacity: there is no system that explains why a listing in one neighbourhood commands twice the nightly rate of a similar property two kilometres away, and what specific attributes drive that premium. Second, regulatory blindness: a wave of new STR legislation across European cities — Paris's 90-night annual cap, Athens's central district registration freeze, Barcelona's 2024 new-license ban — is reshaping investment risk without accessible, queryable intelligence to quantify it.

The result: investors either overpay for overcrowded markets or miss emerging opportunities, not because the data does not exist, but because there is no system that reasons across it, explains its conclusions, and delivers them in a format a decision-maker can act on.

🔍

Pricing opacity

Listing prices vary 3–5× within the same neighbourhood with no transparent, data-backed explanation of the drivers. Hosts underprice; investors miscalculate yield.

⚠️

Regulatory blindness

STR regulation is fragmented, city-specific, and changing rapidly. Investors cannot easily quantify how a new 90-night cap in Paris or a license freeze in Athens affects their projected returns.

📉

Supply health risk

Host churn and platform quality decay are invisible until they affect occupancy. There is no early-warning signal for which markets are losing high-quality supply — a critical leading indicator for investors.

Our answer: ARIA

ARIA — Agentic Real-estate Intelligence Advisor — is a multi-agent AI system purpose-built for short-term rental investment intelligence. It ingests structured listing data, time-series occupancy signals, review sentiment, and regulatory documents, then deploys four specialist AI agents to reason across them and synthesise a governed, explainable investment recommendation. It does not produce a dashboard. It produces a decision.

Research question

Given two structurally different European STR markets — one mature and regulated (Paris), one fast-growing with emerging regulatory risk (Athens) — can a multi-agent AI system reason across pricing dynamics, occupancy forecasts, host risk signals, and regulatory constraints to generate an explainable, evidence-based investment recommendation?

Hypothesis

A governed LangGraph-orchestrated multi-agent system combining XGBoost pricing prediction, Prophet occupancy forecasting, LightGBM host risk classification, and RAG-based regulatory intelligence will produce investment recommendations that are more explainable and more comprehensive than any single-model or single-data-source approach.

◈ Why this matters to KPMG Lighthouse specifically

KPMG's Lighthouse division is explicitly mandated to turn AI ambition into scalable business value — and to do so in a governed, explainable, and compliant way. ARIA is not a chatbot. It is a governed AI system that reasons, retrieves, evaluates, explains, and acts across multiple workflows — the exact language from the KPMG brief (slide 12). Every component maps directly to a Lighthouse capability: the pricing agent maps to Advanced Analytics, the RAG compliance layer maps to Trusted AI & Governance, the orchestrator maps to AI Agents, and the Streamlit demo maps to New Platform Lab.

Beyond the technical alignment, the real estate investment context gives KPMG a genuine consulting angle: this system is a prototype for a deal-advisory tool they could offer institutional STR fund managers. That is a real product, not a student exercise.

Section 02 — System Overview

What ARIA is and how it works

A layered architecture — data foundation to agentic interface — where each layer is independently valuable but becomes greater than the sum of its parts when connected through the LangGraph orchestrator.

Layer 1
Data

Dual-source data foundation

Why two sources? The Kaggle "Airbnb Prices in European Cities" dataset gives us 51,707 pre-cleaned listings with pricing and property features — we can run ML on day one. Inside Airbnb gives us what Kaggle lacks: review text for NLP sentiment, calendar.csv for occupancy time-series, and GeoJSON for maps. Together they cover every dimension ARIA needs.

Key datasets: paris_weekdays.csv, paris_weekends.csv, athens_weekdays.csv, athens_weekends.csv (Kaggle) + listings.csv.gz, calendar.csv.gz, reviews.csv.gz, neighbourhoods.geojson (Inside Airbnb, Athens Sept 2025 + Paris).

Kaggle — 51,707 listings Inside Airbnb — CC BY 4.0 pandas / geopandas
Layer 2
ML Models

Three independently trained, agent-callable models

Why three models and not one? Because pricing, occupancy, and risk are orthogonal signals. A single model trying to predict all three produces worse estimates than three specialists. Each model is independently evaluated, independently testable, and exposed to the LangGraph orchestrator as a typed tool call — which also means any one model can be swapped without rebuilding the agent.

Model A — XGBoost + SHAP: Predicts log(realsum). Optuna-tuned, 50 trials. SHAP TreeExplainer produces per-listing force plots and natural-language explanations. Model B — Prophet forecaster: Monthly occupancy proxy from calendar.csv. Weekday/weekend regressors. 12-month horizon. Model C — LightGBM classifier: Host risk score 0–1 from superhost status, multi-listing flags, business flag, satisfaction scores, and VADER sentiment from reviews.

XGBoost + SHAP Prophet LightGBM VADER sentiment Optuna · scikit-learn
Layer 3
RAG

Regulatory knowledge retrieval

Why RAG and not fine-tuning? Regulations change. Fine-tuned models go stale. A RAG system over a maintained ChromaDB vector store can be updated with a new document in minutes, and every answer cites the specific retrieved passage — which is essential for the compliance use case where auditability matters. We chunk Paris (Loi Le Meur, 90-night cap, SIRET registration) and Athens (AMA system, central district freeze from Dec 2024, October 2025 safety standards) regulation texts, embed with OpenAI text-embedding-3-small, and retrieve top-3 chunks per investor query.

ChromaDB LangChain OpenAI embeddings semantic chunking
Layer 4
Agents

LangGraph supervisor + 4 specialist agents

Why LangGraph and not a single LLM prompt? A monolithic prompt with all the tools available degrades rapidly as context grows — the model starts ignoring tools, confusing their outputs, and generating hallucinated explanations. LangGraph's graph-based state machine keeps each agent's scope bounded: the Pricing Agent only calls the XGBoost tool, the Compliance Agent only queries ChromaDB. The supervisor routes based on intent classification. Shared TypedDict state means every agent reads the full conversation context but writes only to its own output field.

Four specialist agents: Pricing Agent (calls XGBoost tool, formats SHAP output) → Forecast Agent (calls Prophet tool, produces revenue scenario) → Risk Agent (calls LightGBM tool, produces neighbourhood risk score) → Compliance Agent (queries ChromaDB, cites retrieved regulation text). Supervisor → Synthesis Agent generates the final investor memo draft → simulated HITL approval button in Streamlit.

LangGraph Supervisor pattern TypedDict state OpenAI GPT-4o-mini tool calling
Layer 5
Interface

Streamlit demo + PDF investor brief

Why Streamlit and not a custom React frontend? Streamlit ships in hours not days, and for a capstone demo that runs once in front of a panel, reliability and speed of iteration matter more than pixel-perfect UI. Two-column layout: left side is the investor chat input; right side is a live reasoning panel that updates as each agent completes — SHAP waterfall (Plotly), 12-month forecast chart, Folium neighbourhood heatmap, and the generated memo draft. The "approve" button simulates the HITL governance checkpoint.

The investor brief is generated as a ReportLab PDF with six structured sections — the kind of document a KPMG analyst would hand to a client. This is what makes the demo memorable: the investor asks a question and receives a formatted PDF in response.

Section 03 — City Selection

Paris × Athens — justified

City selection is not aesthetic. Each city must create analytical tension that makes the investor question non-trivial. These two cities do exactly that.

Mature Market · Regulatory Stability

Paris

45,098active listings
€164avg. daily rate
80%occupancy rate
€49Kmedian annual host revenue

Investor thesis: the market is deep and data-rich but returns are compressing. The 90-night annual cap (primary residence), Loi Le Meur (November 2024), mandatory DPE energy rating, and the ability of co-ownership syndicates to ban STR by two-thirds vote create a complex compliance surface. The question is not whether to invest but where and in what property type to avoid regulatory exposure while capturing yield above the neighbourhood median.

Explosive Growth · Regulatory Risk Rising

Athens

12,626active listings
€81avg. daily rate
71%occupancy rate
+22.9%YoY supply growth 2025

Investor thesis: Greece recorded Europe's highest STR booking growth in 2025 (+45% YoY). Athens specifically added 2,074 new listings in 2025. But from December 2024, new AMA registrations in central Athens's 1st–3rd municipal districts are frozen for properties not previously registered. October 2025 introduced mandatory safety standards (AC, ventilation, liability insurance). The window is narrowing. The question is whether to enter now in peripheral districts, or wait for the regulatory picture to clarify — with quantified downside scenarios.

Why this pairing is analytically correct

The contrast is structural, not superficial. Paris has 3.6× more listings, 2× the ADR, but near-saturated supply and tightening regulation — an optimisation problem. Athens has explosive supply growth, lower ADR with upside potential, and a regulatory window that is actively closing — an entry-timing problem. These two problems require different analytical tools (price optimisation vs occupancy forecasting and regulatory risk quantification), which is exactly why building one system that handles both demonstrates genuine technical breadth to KPMG. A team that chose two similar markets would have nothing interesting to compare.

Weekend vs weekday: the free analytical gift

The Kaggle dataset splits every city into weekday and weekend CSVs. Merging them with an is_weekend binary feature gives us price elasticity analysis for free. Preliminary expectation: Athens will show higher weekend price premiums (leisure-driven demand) while Paris will show flatter elasticity (year-round professional and tourist mix). This is one additional insight slide in the KPMG presentation that costs zero extra work.

Confirmed column schema — Kaggle dataset (20 columns)

These are the exact columns available, confirmed from the Baselight SQL schema dump of the Kaggle dataset. Every ML feature maps to one of these.

realsum

FLOAT — Target variable

Total price EUR for 2 people, 2 nights. Log-transform for normality before modelling. This is what we predict.

room_type

VARCHAR — Categorical

Entire home / Private room / Shared room. One-hot encode. Largest price segmentation variable.

person_capacity

FLOAT — Numerical

Maximum guests. Strong positive price predictor. Engineer bedrooms_per_person ratio.

host_is_superhost

BOOLEAN — Binary

Platform quality signal. Use as pricing feature and as anchor for host risk labelling.

multi

INT — Binary flag

1 = host operates multiple listings. Core feature for host risk classifier. Correlates with professional/commercial operation.

biz

INT — Binary flag

1 = business-style host. Combine with multi for operator classification.

cleanliness_rating

FLOAT — 1 to 10

Guest-reported cleanliness. Used in host risk model and as VADER validation cross-check.

guest_satisfaction_overall

FLOAT — 1 to 100

Overall satisfaction score. Primary signal for at-risk host labelling when combined with trend data.

bedrooms

INT — Numerical

Strong structural predictor. Engineer bedrooms × room_type interaction.

dist

FLOAT — km

Distance to city centre. Non-linear price effect — bin into zones (0–1km, 1–3km, 3–5km, 5km+).

metro_dist

FLOAT — km

Distance to nearest metro. Stronger predictor in Paris (dense metro) than Athens (less dense).

attr_index_norm

FLOAT — 0 to 100

Tourist attraction proximity index, normalised. Engineered feature from original source. Combine with rest_index for "lifestyle score".

rest_index_norm

FLOAT — 0 to 100

Restaurant proximity index, normalised. Strong predictor in Paris where dining culture drives premium pricing.

lat / lng

FLOAT — Coordinates

Used for Folium map visualisation and spatial clustering by neighbourhood. Not used as raw ML features.

Section 05 — Technical Architecture

How the system is built

Every architectural choice below is justified. There are no arbitrary technology selections. The justification column is what you say when a KPMG panellist asks "why did you use X instead of Y?"

ComponentTechnologyJustification
Price predictorXGBoost + Optuna + SHAPXGBoost consistently outperforms linear models on tabular real estate data (R² ~0.72–0.99 in published studies). Optuna provides principled hyperparameter search. SHAP is non-negotiable — it transforms a black-box model into an explainable, investor-readable output. Without SHAP, the pricing agent answers "what" but not "why."
Occupancy forecasterProphet (Meta)Prophet handles missing data and irregular seasonality natively — critical for STR data where calendar.csv has gaps. It produces confidence intervals by default, which map directly to the "base / downside scenario" we need in the investor brief. Alternatives like ARIMA require stationarity testing and manual seasonality specification — too slow for a 4-week build.
Host risk classifierLightGBM + VADERLightGBM trains faster than XGBoost on smaller datasets and handles class imbalance well. At-risk hosts are a minority class — we need F1-weighted evaluation, not accuracy. VADER is chosen over BERT because it runs without GPU, ships in 2 hours, and is sufficient for the polarity signal we need from short review texts. BERT would take a week to fine-tune for marginal gain.
Compliance retrievalLangChain + ChromaDBChromaDB runs locally with zero infrastructure overhead — critical for a student project without cloud credits. LangChain provides the retrieval chain abstraction so the compliance agent is 30 lines of code. RAG is chosen over fine-tuning because regulations change; a RAG system can be updated with a new document in 5 minutes.
Agent orchestrationLangGraph (supervisor)LangGraph's graph-based state machine keeps each agent's scope bounded, preventing context overflow that degrades monolithic prompts. The TypedDict shared state gives every agent full conversation context while enforcing output schema. Chosen over CrewAI because LangGraph gives finer control over routing logic and interrupt nodes.
Demo interfaceStreamlitStreamlit ships in hours. For a single live presentation, reliability and iteration speed matter more than pixel-perfect UI. The chat interface, real-time panel updates, and Folium map all render natively in Streamlit without custom JavaScript.
PDF outputReportLabProduces structured, styled PDFs programmatically. The investor brief PDF is generated on-demand from the agent's synthesis output — the kind of deliverable that turns a demo into a real product prototype.
LLM backboneOpenAI GPT-4o-miniCost-effective for the agent reasoning layer (routing, natural language formatting). GPT-4o-mini is sufficient for the synthesis and SHAP explanation tasks. GPT-4o would be used only for the final investor memo synthesis where output quality matters most.
Agent interaction flow

How a single investor query travels through ARIA from input to memo:

# Investor types: "Compare Paris and Athens for a €800K STR investment. Which neighbourhood?" Supervisor Agent (intent classification) → detects: pricing + forecast + risk + compliance + synthesis → sets state: {city_a: "paris", city_b: "athens", mode: "comparison"} → routes to Pricing Agent → calls xgboost_tool(city="paris"), xgboost_tool(city="athens") → formats SHAP output: "Top Paris price driver: tourist attraction proximity (+€28/night)" → writes to state.pricing_output → routes to Forecast Agent → calls prophet_tool(city="paris", horizon=12) → produces scenario: base=€49K/yr, downside (90-night cap enforced)=€38K/yr → writes to state.forecast_output → routes to Risk Agent → calls lightgbm_tool(city="athens") → neighbourhood risk map: Kolonaki=0.82 (high), Exarcheia=0.31 (low) → writes to state.risk_output → routes to Compliance Agent → queries ChromaDB: "Athens registration freeze central districts" → retrieves: "AMA registration frozen in 1st–3rd districts from Dec 2024..." → writes to state.compliance_output → routes to Synthesis Agent → reads all four state fields → generates investor memo draft → HITL checkpoint: presenter clicks "Approve" → PDF generated via ReportLab + displayed in Streamlit

Section 06 — Build Instructions

Step-by-step: how to build ARIA

Specific, executable instructions. Not "set up a model" but exactly what commands to run, what files to create, and what the output should look like. Follow in order.

ℹ️ Prerequisites: Python 3.11+, Git, a shared GitHub repo, OpenAI API key (budget ~$10 total for 4 weeks of dev + demo). Use a virtual environment. Install all dependencies in requirements.txt from day one — everyone uses the same environment.
Phase 1 — Data pipeline (Week 1, Member 1 + Member 2)
  • 1
    Download and merge the Kaggle dataset

    Download all four CSVs: paris_weekdays.csv, paris_weekends.csv, athens_weekdays.csv, athens_weekends.csv. Add columns: city = "paris" | "athens" and is_weekend = 0 | 1. Concatenate all four into a single df_combined.parquet — 5,100 rows for Athens, approx. 9,000 for Paris (both weekday + weekend). Save as Parquet for fast reloading.

  • 2
    Download Inside Airbnb files

    Run: wget "https://data.insideairbnb.com/greece/attica/athens/2025-09-26/data/listings.csv.gz" -O data/raw/athens_listings_full.csv.gz. Repeat for calendar.csv.gz, reviews.csv.gz, and neighbourhoods.geojson. Do the same for Paris (check insideairbnb.com for the latest Paris snapshot URL). Decompress with gunzip. Store in data/raw/. Do not modify raw files — always transform into a new file.

  • 3
    Feature engineering on the Kaggle dataset

    Apply to df_combined: (a) log_price = np.log1p(realsum) — target variable. (b) dist_zone = pd.cut(dist, bins=[0,1,3,5,np.inf], labels=['center','near','mid','far']). (c) lifestyle_score = (attr_index_norm + rest_index_norm) / 2. (d) capacity_per_bedroom = person_capacity / bedrooms.clip(lower=1). (e) One-hot encode room_type and dist_zone. Save as data/processed/df_model_ready.parquet. Run df.isnull().sum() to confirm zero nulls on all modelling columns.

  • 4
    EDA notebook — required outputs

    Produce these specific charts (Member 1 owns, team reviews): price distribution by city (violin plot, log scale), price by room type per city (grouped bar), correlation heatmap of all numerical features, dist vs log_price scatter with regression line per city, top-20 neighbourhoods by median price per city (horizontal bar). These charts are presentation assets — design them cleanly from day one.

Phase 2 — ML models (Week 2, Members 2 + 3)
  • 5
    XGBoost price predictor (Member 2)

    Features: [bedrooms, person_capacity, host_is_superhost, multi, biz, cleanliness_rating, guest_satisfaction_overall, dist, metro_dist, attr_index_norm, rest_index_norm, is_weekend, room_type_encoded, dist_zone_encoded, lifestyle_score, capacity_per_bedroom]. Target: log_price. Train/test split: 80/20, stratified by city. Run Optuna for 50 trials minimising RMSE on validation set. Evaluate on held-out test: report RMSE, MAE, R² separately for Paris and Athens. Then: explainer = shap.TreeExplainer(model). Write a function explain_listing(listing_row) → str that returns: "This listing's predicted price is X. The three biggest drivers are: [feature A] added €Y because it is in the top 20% for [metric], [feature B] reduced price by €Z because..."

  • 6
    Prophet occupancy forecaster (Member 3)

    Process calendar.csv.gz for Athens: parse date column, create booked = (available == 'f').astype(int). Group by listing_id and month to get monthly occupancy rate per listing. Average across all listings per city per month to get city-level monthly occupancy time series. Feed to Prophet: ds = month, y = occupancy_rate. Add is_weekend as external regressor using the Kaggle weekday/weekend price ratio as a proxy. Forecast 12 months forward. Plot with model.plot(forecast). Produce a scenario table: base forecast, optimistic (+10% if regulation stable), downside (−15% if regulatory restriction imposed). This table goes directly into the investor brief.

  • 7
    LightGBM host risk classifier (Member 3)

    Label creation (no ground truth needed — define at-risk heuristically): at_risk = 1 if guest_satisfaction_overall < 70 OR (multi == 1 AND host_is_superhost == False) OR (biz == 1 AND cleanliness_rating < 7). This creates a ~15–25% positive class, which is realistic for platform health. For the Inside Airbnb reviews, run SentimentIntensityAnalyzer().polarity_scores(review_text)['compound'] per review, average per host, and add as feature avg_sentiment_score. Train LightGBM classifier with class_weight='balanced'. Evaluate: F1-weighted, precision, recall, AUC-ROC. Output: risk_score per listing (model probability). Aggregate to neighbourhood median for the Folium heatmap.

  • 8
    Tool contracts — critical handoff to Member 5

    Each model must be wrapped as a Python function with a typed signature. Write these before Member 5 starts LangGraph. Example: def predict_price(city: str, bedrooms: int, person_capacity: int, room_type: str, dist: float, metro_dist: float, attr_index_norm: float, rest_index_norm: float) → dict returning {"predicted_price_eur": float, "shap_explanation": str, "confidence_interval": [float, float]}. Commit these to src/tools/. Member 5 imports them — never calling the model directly.

Phase 3 — RAG pipeline (Week 2, Member 4)
  • 9
    Scrape and prepare regulation documents

    Collect text on: Paris — Loi Le Meur (Nov 2024), the 90-night primary residence cap, SIRET registration requirement, co-ownership ban mechanism, DPE rating requirement. Athens — AMA registration system, central district freeze (1st–3rd districts, from Dec 2024), October 2025 mandatory safety standards (AC, ventilation, liability insurance), running costs (€700–€1,250/month). Save each as a plain .txt file in data/regulations/. These can be copy-pasted from the sources we already found — no scraping framework needed at this scale.

  • 10
    Build ChromaDB vector store

    Use LangChain's RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter(chunk_size=500, chunk_overlap=50) to chunk each document. Embed with OpenAIEmbeddings(model="text-embedding-3-small"). Persist to data/chromadb/. Test with 5 sample questions: "What is the maximum nights per year allowed in Paris?", "Can I register a new Airbnb in central Athens in 2025?", "What insurance is required for Athens STRs?", "What is the SIRET registration requirement in Paris?", "What happens if I exceed the 90-night cap in Paris?" — all five should retrieve the correct passage as the top result. Log precision@1 for each.

  • 11
    Compliance agent function

    Wrap retrieval in a typed function: def query_compliance(query: str, city: str) → dict returning {"answer": str, "sources": list[str], "risk_level": "low" | "medium" | "high"}. The risk level is a heuristic: if retrieved text mentions "freeze", "ban", "penalty", or "illegal" → high. If mentions "registration required" or "cap" → medium. Otherwise → low. Member 5 imports this function as a LangGraph tool node.

Phase 4 — LangGraph orchestrator (Week 3, Member 5)
  • 12
    Define the shared state schema

    Create src/agents/state.py:

    from typing import TypedDict, Optional class ARIAState(TypedDict): query: str city_a: str city_b: str intent: list[str] # ["pricing","forecast","risk","compliance","synthesis"] pricing_output: Optional[dict] forecast_output: Optional[dict] risk_output: Optional[dict] compliance_output: Optional[dict] memo_draft: Optional[str] approved: bool
  • 13
    Build the supervisor routing node

    The supervisor receives the raw query and uses a GPT-4o-mini call with a structured system prompt to classify intent. It returns a list of agent names to invoke. Then LangGraph routes sequentially: pricing → forecast → risk → compliance → synthesis. Use StateGraph(ARIAState), add nodes for each agent, add conditional edges from supervisor output. The supervisor does not do reasoning — it only routes. All reasoning happens inside the specialist agents.

  • 14
    Synthesis agent + HITL simulation

    The synthesis agent reads all four state fields and generates a memo draft using GPT-4o (use the larger model here — this is the output the investor sees). Draft format: City comparison table → Top neighbourhood recommendation → Revenue forecast → Risk flags → Compliance notes → KPMG-style recommendation with confidence level. The HITL checkpoint is simulated in Streamlit: the synthesis agent writes the draft to state, Streamlit displays it with an "Approve and generate PDF" button. On click, ReportLab generates the PDF. This is functionally identical to a real HITL interrupt — it just uses a Streamlit widget instead of a LangGraph interrupt node, which is the right trade-off for a 4-week build.

Model evaluation appendix

XGBoost (pricing): RMSE, MAE, R² on held-out test set, reported separately for Paris and Athens. Include a predicted-vs-actual scatter plot. Discuss where the model over- and under-predicts (likely: luxury outliers and unusual property types). Prophet (forecasting): MAPE at 30-day, 60-day, and 90-day horizons. RAG (compliance): Precision@3 on a held-out set of 10 regulation questions — score how often the correct regulation passage appears in the top 3 retrieved chunks. Target: ≥80% precision@3.

4

Ethics + governance section (5 minutes, presented by M1)

This section is what separates a student project from a KPMG-grade submission. Specifically address: (a) Data bias: the Paris Kaggle subsample is larger than Athens — what does this mean for model confidence on Athens predictions? We acknowledge it and bound our Athens estimates with wider confidence intervals. (b) Label engineering risk: the at-risk host label is heuristic, not ground truth — we are measuring a proxy for churn, not churn itself. (c) Model confidence limits: ARIA should never be used to make a final investment decision without professional due diligence. The disclaimer is in the PDF footer and stated verbally. (d) HITL as governance: the approval checkpoint exists specifically so no AI output reaches the client without human review. This is not a feature — it is a governance principle. KPMG will recognise it as such.

◈ The three questions the KPMG panel will ask — and your answers

"Why XGBoost and not a neural network?" XGBoost with SHAP is the correct choice for tabular data with under 15,000 rows per city. Neural networks require 10–100× more data to outperform gradient-boosted trees on structured tabular inputs. More importantly, SHAP interpretability is non-negotiable for an investor-facing system — a neural network answer without an explanation is a liability in a compliance-adjacent context. The published literature on Airbnb price prediction consistently shows XGBoost and gradient boosting outperform or match more complex approaches at this data scale.

"How do you validate your agent's recommendations?" Three ways. First, the ML models are evaluated on held-out test data with standard metrics logged in the evaluation appendix. Second, the compliance agent outputs cite the specific retrieved regulation passage — every claim is traceable to a source. Third, the synthesis agent's output goes through a HITL approval step — the system explicitly acknowledges it requires human review before the recommendation is acted on.

"What would it take to productionise this?" Three things: replace the Streamlit interface with a FastAPI backend + React frontend; replace the local ChromaDB with a managed vector store (Pinecone or Weaviate); and establish a data refresh pipeline to update Inside Airbnb data quarterly. The LangGraph orchestrator, all three ML models, and the PDF generator are already production-grade in structure. Estimated productionisation timeline from this prototype: 8–12 weeks for a small engineering team.
